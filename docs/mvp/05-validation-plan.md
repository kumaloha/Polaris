# Validation Plan

## Purpose

Define how to test whether the MVP concept is worth building further.

The MVP should validate player desire, not document completeness.

## Core Hypothesis

Female casual players will repeatedly engage with a game where the highest-frequency action is identifying male relationship signals, while self-improvement and relationship investment provide progression and emotional stakes.

## What We Need To Prove

### 1. Case Desire

Question:

- Do players want to read and judge these cases?

Signals:

- They finish multiple cases.
- They discuss or argue about the correct interpretation.
- They want to see the result report.

### 2. Agency Payoff

Question:

- Does choosing a response feel more satisfying than simply identifying the signal?

Signals:

- Players remember their response choice.
- Players replay to try another response.
- Players describe feeling in control.

### 3. Self-Improvement Fit

Question:

- Do persona cards feel like strategy, not decoration?

Signals:

- Players choose different cards for different cases.
- Players understand why a card helped or hurt.
- Players want to upgrade Judgment or Boundary.

### 4. Investment Node Emotion

Question:

- Do low-frequency relationship investment decisions create stronger emotion than normal cases?

Signals:

- Players remember Add/Hold/Fold/Test outcomes.
- Players feel regret, satisfaction, or curiosity.
- Players ask what would have happened if they chose differently.

### 5. Tone Safety

Question:

- Does the game feel sharp without feeling like a hate lecture or manipulation manual?

Signals:

- Players describe it as clever, realistic, fun, or spicy.
- Players do not describe it as preachy, bitter, or exhausting.
- Investment nodes are understood as relationship/emotional investment, not financial or PUA instruction.

## Test Format

### Phase 1: Content-Only Test

Use clickable or document-based case cards before building full code.

Participants:

- 8-12 women from target markets or close proxies.

Test:

- Show 3 cases.
- Ask players to tag signals.
- Show response options.
- Show result reports.
- Ask players to rank which case they would share.

### Phase 2: Low-Fidelity iOS Prototype

Use an iPhone-sized clickable prototype or simple web build.

Participants:

- 12-20 players.

Test:

- 5 playable cases.
- Persona card selection.
- Result report.
- One investment node.

### Phase 3: First Playable MVP

Use a functional vertical prototype.

Participants:

- 50-200 early testers.

Test:

- 10 cases.
- 4 persona cards.
- 3 investment nodes.
- Basic event tracking.

## Qualitative Questions

After a case:

- What do you think he was really doing?
- Did any line feel familiar?
- Which response felt most like something you would want to say?
- Did the result feel fair, surprising, or annoying?
- Would you send this case or result to a friend?

After a persona card:

- Why did you choose that stance?
- Did it feel like a style choice or a strategy choice?
- Would you upgrade Charm, Judgment, Boundary, or Social Rank first?

After an investment node:

- Did you want to Add, Hold, Fold, or Test?
- Did the outcome feel emotionally meaningful?
- Did it feel like relationship strategy inside a game, not real-world manipulation advice?

## Lightweight Metrics

### Case Metrics

- Case start rate.
- Case completion rate.
- Average time per case.
- Signal tag selection rate.
- Response option distribution.
- Result report screenshot/share intent.

### Progression Metrics

- Persona card selection distribution.
- Growth stat upgrade distribution.
- Repeat case attempt rate.

### Investment Metrics

- Portfolio node open rate.
- Add/Hold/Fold/Test distribution.
- Outcome emotional rating.
- Rewind interest.

## Success Thresholds

For a first MVP, strong evidence means:

- At least 70% of testers finish 3 cases.
- At least 50% can explain the game in one sentence.
- At least 40% say they would share a case or result with a friend.
- At least 60% understand persona cards as strategy.
- At least 50% remember the investment node after the session.

These numbers are directional. The main goal is not statistical certainty; it is whether the concept shows enough pull to justify a coded iOS-first prototype.

## Failure Signals

- Players say it feels like a quiz, not a game.
- Players skip result reports.
- Players see self-improvement as unrelated decoration.
- Players read investment as financial or manipulative instruction.
- Players feel the game hates men rather than studies relationship behavior.
- Players want only romance fantasy and reject detection/control gameplay.

## Validation Artifacts

After testing, collect:

- Top 3 most discussed cases.
- Top 3 confusing signals.
- Most shared result line.
- Most polarizing response choice.
- Highest-emotion investment outcome.
- Language players use to describe the game.

## Stop / Continue Rule

Continue if:

- Case desire is strong.
- Result reports create discussion.
- Persona cards support strategy.
- Investment nodes create memory.

Stop or pivot if:

- Players only want passive romance.
- The detection loop feels like homework.
- Tone consistently reads as bitter or instructional.
